Documented: Donald Trump's History of Racial Controversies

By Investigative Staff | April 5, 2025

From discriminatory housing practices in the 1970s to inflammatory rhetoric during his presidency, we examine the documented history of racial controversies surrounding Donald J. Trump.

Former President Donald Trump during his 2024 campaign rally. Photo: ABC News

Donald J. Trump has a long documented history of racial controversies spanning five decades, from his early real estate career to his presidency and beyond. While some supporters dismiss these incidents as misrepresentations, the documented evidence tells a story of persistent racial controversies that have followed Trump throughout his career.

Early Real Estate Career and Housing Discrimination

United States v. Trump Management, Inc. (1973)

In 1973, the Department of Justice filed a civil rights case against Trump Management, Inc., alleging racial discrimination against Black rental applicants. The lawsuit was based on evidence gathered by "testers" from the Urban League, who documented how Black and White applicants were treated differently.

"The government contended that Trump Management had refused to rent or negotiate rentals 'because of race and color.' It also charged that the company had required different rental terms and conditions because of race and that it had misrepresented to blacks that apartments were not available."

Source: The New York Times, "Major Landlord Accused of Antiblack Bias in City," October 16, 1973.

Trump, represented by attorney Roy Cohn, countersued the government for $100 million, claiming the allegations were defamatory. The countersuit was dismissed, and in 1975, Trump Management settled the original case with a consent decree, requiring the company to take steps to ensure compliance with the Fair Housing Act.

Source: The Washington Post, "Inside the government's racial bias case against Donald Trump's company, and how he fought it," January 23, 2016.

Central Park Five Case (1989)

In 1989, a group of five Black and Latino teenagers were falsely accused of assaulting and raping a woman in Central Park. Trump took out full-page advertisements in four New York newspapers calling for the return of the death penalty.

"I want to hate these murderers and I always will. I am not looking to psychoanalyze or understand them, I am looking to punish them."

Source: Donald Trump's advertisement in New York newspapers, May 1989.

The five men were later exonerated by DNA evidence and the confession of the actual perpetrator. Even after their exoneration in 2002, Trump continued to insist that the men were guilty.

Source: USA Today, "Trump still thinks the Central Park Five are guilty," October 7, 2016.

Presidential Campaign and Administration

Campaign Launch and Comments about Mexican Immigrants (2015)

When Trump announced his presidential campaign in June 2015, he made controversial statements about Mexican immigrants that sparked immediate backlash.

"When Mexico sends its people, they're not sending their best... They're bringing drugs. They're bringing crime. They're rapists. And some, I assume, are good people."

Source: Donald Trump's presidential announcement speech, June 16, 2015.

Source: Washington Post

Muslim Ban Proposal (2015)

In December 2015, then-candidate Trump called for "a total and complete shutdown of Muslims entering the United States until our country's representatives can figure out what is going on."

Source: Donald Trump campaign statement, December 7, 2015.

After becoming president, Trump signed Executive Order 13769, which banned entry to the U.S. from seven predominantly Muslim countries. After legal challenges, a modified version was eventually upheld by the Supreme Court.

Source: The New York Times, "Supreme Court Upholds Trump's Travel Ban," June 26, 2018.

Source: New York Times

Comments on Charlottesville "Unite the Right" Rally (2017)

Following a white nationalist rally in Charlottesville, Virginia, where a counter-protester was killed, Trump made controversial comments about "very fine people on both sides."

"You had some very bad people in that group, but you also had people that were very fine people, on both sides."

Source: Donald Trump's remarks to reporters, August 15, 2017.

Source: Politico
July 2019

"Go Back" Tweets about Congresswomen

Trump tweeted that four Democratic congresswomen of color should "go back" to "the totally broken and crime infested places from which they came." Three of the four were born in the United States.

Source: Donald Trump's tweets, July 14, 2019.

June 2020

Black Lives Matter Protests Response

During nationwide protests following George Floyd's killing, Trump referred to protesters as "thugs" and tweeted "when the looting starts, the shooting starts," a phrase with historical racial connotations.

Source: Donald Trump's tweet, May 29, 2020.

Sept 2020

Debate Comments on Proud Boys

When asked to condemn white supremacists during a presidential debate, Trump told the Proud Boys to "stand back and stand by," which was interpreted by many as an endorsement.

Source: First presidential debate transcript, September 29, 2020.

Former White House Staff Allegations

Omarosa Manigault Newman Claims

In her 2018 book "Unhinged," former White House aide Omarosa Manigault Newman claimed that Trump used racial slurs during the filming of "The Apprentice" and that there were tapes to prove it. Trump denied these allegations.

Source: Omarosa Manigault Newman, "Unhinged: An Insider's Account of the Trump White House," 2018.

Source: NPR

Michael Cohen Testimony

Trump's former attorney Michael Cohen testified to Congress in 2019 that Trump had made racist statements in private, including saying that "Black people would never vote for him because they were too stupid."

Source: Michael Cohen testimony before the House Oversight Committee, February 27, 2019.

Source: NBC News

Trump's Defense

"I am the least racist person you have ever interviewed, that I can tell you."
— Donald Trump to reporters, January 14, 2018

Trump has consistently denied allegations of racism and pointed to his administration's policies that he says have benefited minority communities, including:

  • • Criminal justice reform (First Step Act)
  • • Funding for Historically Black Colleges and Universities
  • • Low pre-pandemic unemployment rates for minorities

Source: White House statements, 2017-2021
